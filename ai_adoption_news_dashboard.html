2. Background Theory

Source credibility, machine heuristic, and signaling effect explain why AI roles matter

AI Adoption TypeNo AI, AI-assisted, or AI-written journalism.

Market ReactionInvestors translate credibility and audience response into AR, CAR, volume, and volatility.
Source Credibility

Expertise and Trustworthiness

News credibility depends on whether the source is viewed as competent, accountable, and trustworthy. AI-writing adoption can weaken source credibility if readers perceive the article source as less transparent or less responsible than a human journalist.

Machine Heuristic

Automation as a Mental Shortcut

When audiences see a machine as the content producer, they may use a machine heuristic: AI can be perceived as efficient and data-driven, but also cold, less contextual, and less morally accountable.

Signaling Effect

Investor Interpretation

AI adoption announcements act as market signals about future efficiency, cost structure, innovation capability, and reputational risk. AI-assisted adoption mainly sends an efficiency and complementarity signal, while AI-writing adoption sends a mixed signal: cost reduction potential may be offset by credibility risk and possible reader backlash.

Reader Backlash

Visible Audience Response

Negative comments, social media criticism, search spikes, and low sharing quality can become visible indicators of reputational risk after an AI-writing announcement.

Human-AI Complementarity

Role Boundary Matters

The market reaction should be stronger when AI is framed as supporting journalists and weaker when AI is framed as replacing editorial judgment or news authorship.

AI Adoption Types in News Organizations and Stock Market Reactions

Research question: Do investors respond differently when news organizations adopt AI as an assistant, as a direct writer, or not at all?

Background Theory

Source credibility theory argues that audiences evaluate messages partly by judging the source's expertise, trustworthiness, transparency, and accountability. In journalism, credibility is central because readers expect editorial responsibility, contextual judgment, and correction mechanisms. AI-writing adoption can weaken source credibility when the authorial source becomes unclear or when accountability appears to shift from human editors to an automated system.

Machine heuristic refers to a mental shortcut in which audiences use the fact that a machine produced or selected information as a cue. This cue can work in two directions. It may imply efficiency, consistency, and data-driven processing, but it may also imply lack of empathy, weak contextual judgment, and limited moral accountability. Therefore, the machine heuristic helps explain why AI-assisted journalism may be interpreted differently from AI-written journalism.

Together, source credibility, machine heuristic, and signaling effect connect audience psychology to market response. Signaling effect frames each AI announcement as information that investors use to infer future efficiency, innovation capability, labor substitution, credibility risk, and expected cash-flow consequences. If investors expect AI-writing adoption to damage credibility or trigger visible reader backlash, CAR may be lower than for AI-assisted adoption.

Empirical Design

The main event study estimates abnormal returns around AI adoption announcements. Event windows include [0,+1], [-1,+1], [-3,+3], [-5,+5], and [+2,+10].

CAR_i = alpha + beta1 AI_Assist_i + beta2 AI_Writing_i + beta3 SourceCredibility_i + beta4 MachineHeuristic_i + beta5 SignalingEffect_i + gamma X_i + epsilon_i

Expected Results

AI-assisted adoption is expected to generate positive CAR through a favorable signaling effect based on efficiency, innovation capability, and human-AI complementarity. AI-writing adoption is expected to produce lower CAR when source credibility declines or negative machine heuristics dominate.
